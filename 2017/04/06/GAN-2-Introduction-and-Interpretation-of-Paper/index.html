GAN (2): Introduction via the Original Paper

Apr 6, 2017

GAN (Generative Adversarial Network) has been a hot area of AI research for the past few years. To learn the theory of GAN, it’s the straightforward way to read the paper by Ian Goodfellow (arxiv Generative Adversarial Networks where GAN was put forward.

Two-player Game

The following part in abstract tells that two different models are used in GAN. The differences between these two models have been discussed in the previous article.

… we simultaneously train two models: a generative model $G$ that captures the data distribution, and a discriminative model $D$ that estimates the probability that a sample came from the training data rather than G…

… In the space of arbitrary functions $G$ and $D$, a unique solution exists, with $G$ recovering the training data distribution and $D$ equal to $\frac{1}{2}$ everywhere….

Ian gives an analogy with minimax two-player game. They archive their goals as adversaries. $G$ decoys while $D$ distinguishes. And $\frac{1}{2}$ means that $D$ couldn’t distinguish, in other word, $G$ generates mixes the false with the genuine.

Non-zero-sum

So far, the most striking successes in deep learning have involved discriminative models, usually those that map a high-dimensional, rich sensory input to a class label.

These striking successes have primarily been based on the backpropagation and dropout algorithms, using piecewise linear units which have a particularly well-behaved gradient.

As discussed in previous article, discriminative model does better than generative model in actual result with help of backpropagation and dropout algorithm. However, the generative model compute the joint probability distribution ${\rm P}(X,C)$ which could provide more information about data.

Deep generative models have had less of an impact, due to the difficulty of approximating many intractable probabilistic computations that arise in maximum likelihood estimation and related strategies, and due to difficulty of leveraging the benefits of piecewise linear units in the generative context.

Therefore, they try to combine the two models to improve the result of generative model.

Rule

The derivation process of the GAN formulas is developed in this part. Assume $x$ is sample from data and $z$ is sample from noise. $G(z)$ is the generated result (image, texts, …). $D(x)$ represents the probability that $x$ comes from the real data. Thus, $D$ is trained to maximize the probability of assigning the correct label to both training examples and samples from $G$. While $G$ is train to minimize $\log (1-D(G(z)))$.

This is the value function for GAN:
$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\min \limits_{G} \max \limits_{D} V(D,G) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\mathrm{data}(x)} [ \log D(x) ] + \mathbb{E}_{z\sim p_\mathrm{z}(z)} [ \log (1-D(G(z))) ]
\end{eqnarray}
$$

In this equation, $G$ and $D$ play in these rules:

  • $G$ aims to generate results as closer to real data as possible. Thus $G$ maximizes $D(G(z))$ and minimizes $V(D,G)$
  • $D$ aims to get better at distinguishing the fake results. Thus $D$ maximizes $D(x)$ and $V(D,G)$
